Background
Puerto Rico's electrical grid serves approximately 1.5 million customers across a diverse and geographically challenging island territory. The system comprises over 30,000 miles of transmission and distribution lines, more than three hundred substations, and a generation fleet transitioning from fossil fuels toward renewable energy sources. LUMA Energy assumed management of the transmission and distribution system in June 2021, inheriting decades of deferred maintenance and the devastating impacts of Hurricanes Irma and Maria.
Since assuming operations, LUMA has undertaken a comprehensive grid modernization program aimed at improving reliability, hardening infrastructure against future storms, and accelerating the integration of solar, wind, and battery storage resources. These efforts are guided by Puerto Rico's Act 17-2019, which mandates 100% renewable energy by 2050, with interim targets of 40% by 2025 and 60% by 2040.
The operational environment in Puerto Rico presents unique challenges including tropical weather exposure, mountainous terrain, aging infrastructure, and evolving regulatory requirements. Understanding this context is essential for evaluating performance metrics and prioritizing capital investments.
Salinas BESS is a large-scale, grid-connected battery energy storage facility in Salinas, Puerto Rico, with advanced lithium-ion technology, high efficiency, and rapid response capabilities. It plays a critical role in supporting renewable energy integration, grid stability, and providing ancillary services to PREPA.

CPP is a defined term in the Energy Storage Services Agreement (ESSA) between the Puerto Rico Electric Power Authority (PREPA) and Clean Flexible Energy LLC.
CPP is used to calculate the Monthly Fixed Payment paid to the Resource Provider for making contracted energy storage capability available to PREPA.
CPP combines project (facility) costs and interconnection costs.

Facility Availability Adjustment Calculation
The FAA is determined by the percentage of time the facility is available to deliver energy or accept charge energy, as measured against the contractual requirements.
If the facility's availability meets or exceeds a threshold (e.g., 98%), the FAA is set to 100%.
If availability falls below this threshold, the FAA is reduced according to a formula specified in the contract, which may include further reductions for lower availability levels, as such:
FAA = 100% if Facility Availability ≥ 98%
FAA = 100% - [(98% - Facility Availability) × 2] if Facility Availability is between 70% and 98%
FAA = 0% if Facility Availability < 70% (unless otherwise specified for Force Majeure or other exceptions)
Source:  Appendix F – Compensation – Monthly Contract Capability – Pages 116-119

PREPA Risk Adjustment
The PRA ensures that the provider is not penalized for hours when PREPA-related risks (such as grid events, force majeure, or insurable interruptions) prevent the delivery of energy storage services.
It proportionally reduces the payment for the billing period based on the number of hours affected by PREPA Risk Events.
The PRA is part of the compensation structure to fairly allocate risk between PREPA and the Resource Provider.
Source:  Appendix F – Compensation – PREPA Risk Adjustment – Pages 119-120

PFHRSn
Duration (in hours) of any Force Majeure affecting PREPA occurring in the billing period, subject to a maximum defined as the Force Majeure Waiting Period.
IPHRSn
Duration (in hours) of any event during the billing period in respect of which the Resource Provider may recover insurance proceeds from any insurance policy obtained in respect of PREPA Risk Events, including business interruption insurance.

Continuous Operation
Operation continuously in Discharge Mode, Charge Mode, or Storage Mode very hour of every billing period, as directed by dispatch notices or charge notices.
Discharge Mode
The operator shall, while in Discharge Mode, make available energy corresponding to the Design Duration Energy at Dmax (maximum design duration energy—the maximum amount of energy deliverable over the design duration), plus Ancillary Services (support services that maintain grid reliability and are defined in the Minimum Technical Requirements or MTRs), at the Interconnection point.
Storage Mode
While in Storage Mode, make available storage of energy corresponding to the Design Storage Energy.
Charge Mode
While in Charge Mode, accept energy at the Charge Point corresponding to the Design Charge Energy during the Design Charge Duration.

Scheduled Maintenance
Source:  Section 6.3 – Operation of the Facility – Scheduled Maintenance – Page 50-52
Scheduled Outages or Deratings
Maximum of 160 hours of scheduled outages or deratings per agreement year.
Unused outages do not carry over.
Scheduled Maintenance Program Submission
Scheduled Maintenance Program must be submitted to PREPA at least 60 days prior to the Commercial Operation Date and by September 1 of each subsequent year.
SMP Components
Scheduled Maintenance Program must include the description of work, approximate start and end dates and times, time to restore and for scheduled deratings, energy storage services available during such event.
SMP Review Period
PREPA has 30 days to accept the program or request rescheduling.
If PREPA fails to respond within the 30 days, the SMP is considered accepted.
SMP Rescheduling Accommodations
Resource provider uses reasonable efforts to accommodate rescheduling.
If resource provider is unable to meet requested dates, the provider will propose alternatives for PREPA to select.
SMP Notification
Resource provider notifies PREPA at least seven days prior to any scheduled outage or derating.
SMP Near Term Revisions
Resource provider bears PREPA’s costs for revisions, except force majeure or PREPA risk events, for changes that are less than 60 days prior to the start.
SMP Timelines and Approval
Only outages that meet submittal timelines and approved by PREPA will be counted as scheduled outages or deratings.
Unscheduled Outages & Deratings
Source:  Section 6.4 – Operation of the Facility – Non-Scheduled Outages & Deratings – Page 52
Coordination
If the Resource Provider determines that a NonScheduled Outage or NonScheduled Derating is necessary, it must coordinate the timing of that outage or derating with PREPA.
Notification
Resource Provider shall use commercially reasonable efforts to notify PREPA of any Non‑Scheduled Outage or Non‑Scheduled Derating by 17:00 (Puerto Rico time) on the third Business Day before the affected Day.
If an unexpected Non‑Scheduled Outage or Non‑Scheduled Derating occurs, Resource Provider shall notify PREPA by telephone or email as soon as reasonably practicable and, except in cases of Force Majeure, within fifteen (15) minutes of the event.
As soon as reasonably practicable thereafter, Resource Provider shall provide written notice to PREPA including:
the event or condition,
the date and time it occurred,
the expected end date and time,
for Non‑Scheduled Deratings, the Energy Storage Services available during the event, and
any other information requested by PREPA.
Lack of Notification
Notwithstanding any notice or coordination with PREPA, the Facility shall be deemed unavailable for the full duration of a NonScheduled Outage for purposes of calculating Facility Availability.
Supply Period Performance Tests
Source:  Section 6.9 – Operation of the Facility – Supply Period Performance Tests – Pages 55-56
Number of Tests
During each Agreement Year of the Supply Period, PREPA may require up to two PREPA Performance Tests in accordance with the Testing Protocol.
PREPA’s decision to forgo any test does not waive its right to require future Performance Tests.
Requirements
Resource Provider shall submit to PREPA, for evaluation and approval, all Performance Test reports certified by an experienced and duly qualified independent laboratory or company with specialized expertise in acceptance and related testing of renewable power facilities, demonstrating that the Facility meets all MTRs and the Performance Guarantee.
PREPA may approve or reject the proposed laboratory or company, provided such approval is withheld or delayed.
For clarity, Resource Provider acknowledges that PREPA will not accept manufacturers’ test reports as evidence of compliance.
Coordination
Resource Provider shall coordinate all Performance Tests with PREPA’s personnel and the Independent Expert, who shall witness the tests.
Resource Provider shall provide PREPA with at least thirty (30) Days’ prior written notice of all Performance Tests, field tests, or other matters PREPA may witness.
The Parties shall cooperate in good faith to establish mutually acceptable dates for all Performance Tests.
Additional Tests
Resource Provider may request an additional Performance Test (“Resource Provider Performance Test”) if (i) a Performance Test results in a downward adjustment to the Monthly Contract Capability under Appendix F (Compensation), (ii) a Scheduled Outage has been completed, or (iii) the results of a PREPA Performance Test fall outside the Testing Protocol standards.
The Resource Provider Performance Test shall begin no later than ten (10) Business Days after the PREPA Performance Test showing the low results or after completion of the Scheduled Outage.
Resource Provider shall conduct the test in accordance with the Testing Protocol, except that (A) Resource Provider shall notify PREPA of its request and proposed start and end times at least three (3) Business Days before commencement, and (B) PREPA, in its sole discretion, shall either approve the proposal or provide two (2) alternative start and stop times from which Resource Provider may choose.
The Tested Duration Energy determined through the Resource Provider Performance Test shall be used to establish the Monthly Contract Capability in the same manner as results from a PREPA Performance Test.
